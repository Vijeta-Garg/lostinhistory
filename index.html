The Structure of DNA & the History of Its Discovery

4.1 — Chemical Basis of Heredity

The study of molecular genetics began in the mid-twentieth century as scientists sought to understand the chemical basis of heredity. Early transformation experiments by Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty established that DNA — not protein — was responsible for transmitting genetic information between generations.

The pivotal breakthrough came in 1953, when James Watson and Francis Crick published their famous double-helix model in Nature. Their model explained how DNA could be replicated faithfully and how genetic information could be encoded in the linear sequence of nucleotide bases.

Key to determining DNA's structure was X-ray crystallography. Diffraction patterns revealed the molecule's helical geometry. Photo 51 — among the clearest crystallographic images of DNA yet produced — provided precise angular measurements and helical parameters that proved decisive in finalizing the model.

4.2 — Chromosomal Sex Determination

The chromosomal basis of sex was established in the early 1900s. Thomas Hunt Morgan used Drosophila to demonstrate that genes are carried on chromosomes, earning the Nobel Prize in 1933. Chromosome studies of the era established that females carry XX and males carry XY.

4.3 — Nuclear Fission & Radioactive Tracers

Atomic physics informed biology through radioactive tracer techniques. Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann achieved nuclear fission in 1938, showing that uranium nuclei could be split — a discovery that opened new methods for labeling and tracking molecules inside living cells.
